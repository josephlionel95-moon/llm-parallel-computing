# References — Chapter 06: Distributed Computing Basics

## Documentation (primary sources — this chapter is systems, so docs outrank papers)

- **PyTorch Distributed overview** —
  https://pytorch.org/docs/stable/distributed.html
  Why read: the authoritative API surface: init_process_group and its
  init_methods (env://, tcp://, file://), backend capability matrix
  (which collective works on NCCL/Gloo/MPI), and the timeout semantics our
  `dist.py` overrides.

- **torchrun (Elastic Launch) docs** —
  https://pytorch.org/docs/stable/elastic/run.html
  Why read: every flag used in this chapter (--standalone, --nnodes,
  --node_rank, --rdzv_backend/endpoint, --max-restarts) with the exact list of
  environment variables the agent sets in each worker.

- **TorchElastic rendezvous design** —
  https://pytorch.org/docs/stable/elastic/rendezvous.html
  Why read: the state machine behind exercise 8 — how membership changes are
  agreed, why the whole group restarts, and what "elastic" really promises.

- **NCCL documentation** — https://docs.nvidia.com/deeplearning/nccl/
  Why read: the environment-variable reference (NCCL_DEBUG, NCCL_SOCKET_IFNAME,
  NCCL_P2P_DISABLE...) and the explanation of transports (P2P, SHM, NET) you
  will see in NCCL_DEBUG=INFO output on Kaggle.

- **"Writing Distributed Applications with PyTorch" tutorial** —
  https://pytorch.org/tutorials/intermediate/dist_tuto.html
  Why read: the classic hands-on walkthrough — point-to-point and collectives
  with mp.spawn, close in spirit to this chapter's notebook.

- **torch.multiprocessing docs** —
  https://pytorch.org/docs/stable/multiprocessing.html
  Why read: spawn vs fork vs forkserver, CUDA's incompatibility with fork, and
  the picklability rules that constrain notebook workers.

- **PyTorch "Distributed Debugging" notes** —
  https://pytorch.org/docs/stable/distributed.html#debugging-torch-distributed-applications
  Why read: TORCH_DISTRIBUTED_DEBUG, TORCH_NCCL_BLOCKING_WAIT /
  TORCH_NCCL_ASYNC_ERROR_HANDLING — the exact flags in our failure-modes table,
  with their version-to-version renames.

## Papers

- **Li et al. (2020), "PyTorch Distributed: Experiences on Accelerating Data
  Parallel Training"** — https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.15704
  Why read: §3–4 describe ProcessGroup abstraction and NCCL integration from
  the implementers' side; also the bridge to ch09 (bucketing/overlap).

- **Jeon et al. (2019), "Analysis of Large-Scale Multi-Tenant GPU Clusters for
  DNN Training Workloads" (USENIX ATC)** —
  https://www.usenix.org/conference/atc19/presentation/jeon
  Why read: what actually fails in production GPU clusters and how often —
  the empirical case for elasticity, health checks, and short timeouts.

- **Sergeev & Del Balso (2018), "Horovod: fast and easy distributed deep
  learning in TensorFlow"** — https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.05799
  Why read: the MPI-flavored alternative rendezvous/launch design; comparing
  it with torchrun sharpens what is essential (SPMD, collectives) vs
  incidental (who sets the env vars).

- **NCCL: Jeaugey (2017), "Optimized inter-GPU communication with NCCL 2"
  (GTC talk / NVIDIA developer blog)**
  Why read: rings, trees, and topology detection explained by the author —
  the "why NCCL is fastest" section of this chapter in slide form.

## Code worth reading

- **`torch/distributed/run.py`** (torchrun's source) — the agent/worker split,
  env-var construction, and restart loop in ~1k readable lines.
- **`src/llmdist/utils/dist.py`** (this repo) — the 89-line distillation this
  chapter walks through line by line; everything later imports it.
