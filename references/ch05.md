# References — Chapter 05: Why Multi-GPU? The Three Walls

## Papers

- **Kaplan et al. (2020), "Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models"**
  https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361
  Why read: the paper that made "compute is the currency" quantitative — loss
  as a power law in N, D, and C, and the origin of the C ≈ 6ND accounting
  convention this chapter uses.

- **Hoffmann et al. (2022), "Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models"
  (Chinchilla)**
  https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556
  Why read: the D ≈ 20N rule behind every GPU-years number in this chapter,
  and the demonstration that GPT-3-era models were badly undertrained. §3's
  three estimation approaches are a masterclass in empirical scaling work.

- **Amdahl (1967), "Validity of the single processor approach to achieving
  large scale computing capabilities"**
  https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1465482.1465560
  Why read: three pages from 1967 that still govern your training step. The
  1/s ceiling derivation is ours verbatim.

- **Gustafson (1988), "Reevaluation of Amdahl's Law"**
  https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/42411.42415
  Why read: two pages. The scaled-speedup argument that justifies
  10,000-GPU clusters — and its hidden assumption (the problem grows), which
  the next reference limits.

- **McCandlish et al. (2018), "An Empirical Model of Large-Batch Training"**
  https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.06162
  Why read: the critical batch size — the wall *behind* Gustafson's escape.
  Gradient noise scale predicts when adding data-parallel ranks stops buying
  optimization progress.

- **Brown et al. (2020), "Language Models are Few-Shot Learners" (GPT-3)**
  https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165
  Why read (appendix): the 175B/300B-token data point our "centuries on one
  GPU" derivation is anchored to; the compute they report matches C = 6ND
  within rounding — check it yourself.

- **Touvron et al. (2023), "LLaMA: Open and Efficient Foundation Language
  Models"**
  https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.13971
  Why read: deliberate overtraining past Chinchilla (1T+ tokens at 7B) for
  inference economy — the regime that makes the compute wall worse and
  exercise 7's trade-off concrete.

- **Rajbhandari et al. (2020), "ZeRO"** — https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.02054
  Why read here: Table 1 is our memory-wall table at production scale; the
  paper is the bridge from this chapter's "doesn't fit" to Part IV's cures.

## Documentation & hardware datasheets

- **NVIDIA A100 / H100 / T4 datasheets** (nvidia.com)
  Why read: the peak-FLOPs and memory numbers in `training_calculator.py`.
  Learn to spot the sparsity asterisk — H100's famous 1979 TFLOP/s is 2:4
  structured sparsity; dense bf16 is 989.

- **PaLM paper §B (Chowdhery et al. 2022)** — https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.02311
  Why read: the canonical definition of MFU (Model FLOPs Utilization) and
  reported values at 6144-TPU scale — the source of "40% is a good number".

## Blog posts

- **Epoch AI, "Estimating training compute"** (epochai.org)
  Why read: careful methodology for the 6ND estimate and its failure modes —
  a sanity check on every napkin in this chapter.

- **Karpathy, "A Recipe for Training Neural Networks"** (karpathy.github.io)
  Why read: not about scale, but about why the serial fraction in practice is
  often YOUR debugging loop — the human Amdahl term.
